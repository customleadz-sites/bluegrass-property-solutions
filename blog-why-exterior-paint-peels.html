Why exterior paint peels early — and how to stop it

Paint that fails in two or three years almost never failed because of the paint. Here’s what actually went wrong, and how you spot whether the next quote is going to do the same thing.

A decent exterior paint job should give you somewhere in the region of ten to fifteen years. When one lets go in two, the homeowner’s conclusion is usually “they used cheap paint.”

Occasionally that’s true. Far more often, the paint was fine and it was never given anything solid to hold onto.

Paint doesn’t stick by magic. It forms a mechanical grip on the surface underneath. If that surface is dusty, greasy, damp, glossy or crumbling, the paint grips that — and it can only hold on as well as the thing it’s holding.

Here are the four ways it goes wrong.

1. It went on over chalk and mildew

Run your hand down an older painted wall. If it comes away with a fine powder on it, that’s chalking — the binder in the old paint has broken down under UV and it’s shedding pigment.

Paint over that and you’ve glued your new coat to a layer of loose powder. It’ll look perfect the day it’s finished. It’ll start letting go within a couple of seasons, usually in sheets, because the powder is the weakest link and the whole coat is only as good as the weakest link.

Same story with mildew, and mildew is worse — it’s alive. Seal it under a coat of paint and it keeps growing in the damp dark underneath, pushing the film off the wall from behind. That’s where the bubbling comes from.

The fix: a proper soft wash first, and enough drying time afterwards. Not a rinse — a wash with a solution that kills growth at the root.

2. The loose paint wasn’t taken off

If the old coat is already flaking, painting over it is painting over a surface that is, by definition, already failing.

The new coat can only be as sound as the layer below. Fresh paint on top of failing paint fails on the failing paint’s timetable, not on its own. You didn’t buy fifteen years — you bought a fresh color on top of a countdown that was already running.

The fix: scrape everything loose, then feather the hard edges back with a sander so the transitions disappear. Skip the feathering and you’ll see every old edge telegraphing through the new coat in raking light — which is most of the reason people say a job “looks fine from the road.”

3. Bare wood never got primed

Once you’ve scraped, you’ve got exposed timber. Bare exterior wood is thirsty, and it’s not consistent — it drinks unevenly across the grain.

Put topcoat straight onto it and some of it soaks in, some sits on top, and you end up with wildly different film thicknesses across the same board. The thin bits fail first. Add that weathered wood surface fibers are themselves loose, and you’re back to gluing paint to something that’s already letting go.

The fix: spot-prime every bare area before topcoating. Primer is formulated to do the two jobs topcoat can’t — bite into the timber, and give the paint a consistent surface to grab. It is not an optional step, and it’s the first one that gets quietly dropped when someone is trying to hit a low number.

4. Moisture was trapped underneath

This is the sneaky one, and it’s a real factor in Kentucky.

Paint the wall while there’s still moisture in it and you’ve sealed water inside. The sun heats the wall, that water turns to vapour, the vapour needs somewhere to go, and the only thing in its way is your new paint film. It pushes it off from behind. That’s blistering — and it looks identical to “bad paint” to anybody who wasn’t there when it was applied.

It comes from a few places: painting too soon after a wash, painting too soon after rain, painting late in the day when dew is coming, or a genuine moisture problem in the wall — a leaking gutter, bad grading, a failed seal — which paint will never fix and will only hide for a while.

The fix: let it dry properly, watch the dew point rather than just the temperature, and deal with the actual water source if there is one. Sometimes that means telling you that you have a gutter problem and not a paint problem.

How to tell whether a quote is going to last

You don’t need to know a thing about paint to work this out. Just ask what happens before the paint goes on.

  • Are they washing it, and how long are they leaving it to dry?
  • Are they scraping and sanding, or just “prepping”?
  • Are they priming bare wood — all of it, or the worst of it?
  • How many days is the whole job, and how much of that is prep?

That last one is the most revealing question you can ask. On a proper exterior repaint, prep is usually the majority of the labour. If someone’s quoting you a whole house painted in a day, they haven’t priced any prep — and now you know exactly what you’ll be looking at in two summers.

The cheapest quote frequently is the cheapest quote precisely because the invisible half of the job isn’t in it.
