How to choose exterior paint colors that work

Color is the part of a repaint people lose the most sleep over — and it's the easiest to get right if you work in the correct order. Here's a low-regret way to land on a scheme you'll still like in five years.

People come at exterior color backwards. They fall in love with a chip in the store, paint the whole house, and then discover it reads completely different in daylight against their brick and their roof. Here's the order that avoids that.

1. Start with what you can't change

Your roof, brick or stone, and any permanent stonework are fixed — you're painting around them, not over them. Pull your color from those. A warm brown roof wants warm-leaning body colors; a cool grey roof opens up cooler ones. Fighting your fixed elements is the fastest way to a scheme that never quite settles.

2. Think in threes: body, trim, accent

Most exteriors that look "designed" rather than "painted" use three colors:

  • Body — the main field, the biggest area, usually the most restrained.
  • Trim — fascia, window and door surrounds; typically lighter or darker than the body for contrast and crispness.
  • Accent — the front door, shutters. This is where you're allowed some personality, because it's a small, cheap-to-change area.

Put your boldness in the accent, not the body. A daring front door is charming; a daring whole house is a commitment you'll question by year three.

3. Test big, and test on the real wall

This is the step that saves people. A two-inch chip lies — color looks far more intense over a large area, and completely different in your actual light. Get sample pints, paint a two-foot square on a couple of different walls, and live with it.

4. Look at it at different times of day

Your east wall in morning sun, your north wall in flat shade and the whole house at dusk are three different color experiences. Kentucky and Tennessee's bright summer sun in particular washes light colors out and deepens dark ones. Check your samples morning, midday and evening before you commit.

5. Don't ignore the neighbors — or the HOA

You don't have to match the street, but a color that clashes hard with both houses beside you will stand out in the wrong way and can dent resale. And if you're in a neighborhood with a homeowners' association, check the approved palette before you fall in love with anything — repainting because the HOA said no is an expensive lesson.

6. When in doubt, go a little quieter

Nobody regrets a tasteful, slightly restrained exterior. Plenty of people regret the bold choice that looked great on the sample and loud on the house. If you're torn between two shades, the softer one is almost always the safer bet for a surface this big and this permanent.
