Selling your home? The exterior jobs that pay you back

A buyer forms an opinion of your house before they're out of the car. That first impression is almost entirely exterior — and a handful of jobs move it the most for the least money. Here's where to spend.

Curb appeal isn't a soft, nice-to-have idea — it's the first data point a buyer gets, and it colors everything they think afterward. A tired exterior makes people assume the inside was neglected too; a sharp one makes them walk in already wanting to like it. The good news is that the exterior jobs that move a buyer most are also some of the cheapest on the list.

1. Wash everything first — it's the best value there is

Before you spend a dollar on anything else, have the house, driveway, walkways and deck washed. A soft wash pulls years of grey-green grime off siding, and a pressure wash takes a driveway from charcoal back to concrete. It's the single highest return-per-dollar job in this whole list, because so much of what looks "old" is just dirty. Sometimes a wash is all a house needs to photograph well.

2. Paint what the buyer sees first

A full repaint before selling pays back well when the paint is genuinely failing. But even if the body's fine, freshening the high-impact spots punches above its cost: the front door, the trim, the shutters, the porch. These are small areas that draw the eye and photograph up close — and they're what people stand in front of while the agent finds the key.

3. Sharpen the front entry

The doorway is where the buyer literally pauses. A freshly painted front door in a confident color, clean and re-caulked trim, and crisp lines around it do a disproportionate amount of work. It's the last thing they see before they step inside — make it say "cared for."

4. Bring the deck and fence back

A grey, splintery deck reads as a chore the buyer will inherit. A cleaned and freshly stained one reads as usable outdoor living space — which buyers pay for. Same wood, completely different impression, and a recoat is far cheaper than the deck replacement a buyer will mentally price in when they see bare grey boards.

5. Fix the small stuff that signals neglect

Peeling trim, a bit of bare wood, cracked caulk, a stained soffit — individually minor, but together they whisper "deferred maintenance," and buyers hear it loudly. Knocking out the small exterior fixes removes the excuses a buyer uses to talk the price down.

Spend where the eye lands

You don't have to do everything. If the budget's tight, the order is: wash first, then the front entry, then any obviously failing paint, then the deck. That sequence buys the most first impression per dollar.
