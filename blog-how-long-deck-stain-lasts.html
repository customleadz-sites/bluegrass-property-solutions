How long does deck stain last — and when to recoat?

Anywhere from about one year to five, depending on the stain, the wood, the weather and how much foot traffic and sun it takes. Here's how to read your own deck — including a 30-second test that settles it.

It's the question every deck owner eventually asks, usually while looking at boards that have gone grey and wondering if they left it too long. The good news: there's a dead-simple test that tells you the answer. But first, why the range is so wide.

What decides how long it lasts

The type of stain is the biggest factor:

  • Clear sealers — the shortest, often just a year, because they offer little UV protection.
  • Semi-transparent stains — usually two to three years on a deck.
  • Solid-color stains — the longest, often three to five years, because the pigment blocks more sun.

Then three more things move it up or down:

  • Horizontal vs. vertical. Deck boards take standing water, sun and every footstep, so they wear fastest. A fence or railing — vertical, shedding water, never walked on — can last twice as long on the same stain.
  • Sun and shade. UV is what breaks stain down. A deck baking in full southern sun gives up its finish years before a shaded one.
  • Our climate. Kentucky's humidity and heavy rain are hard on wood finishes. Wet, humid conditions wear a coating faster and drive moisture into any gap it can find, so expect the shorter end of each range here rather than the longer.

The 30-second test that settles it

Forget the calendar. Sprinkle a cup of water across a few different boards and watch:

  • Water beads up and rolls off? Your finish is still working. Leave it.
  • Water soaks in and darkens the wood within a minute or two? The stain has stopped repelling water — it's time to recoat.

Do this on the sunniest, most-walked area, because that's where the finish fails first. If that spot still beads, you've got time.

Other signs it's due

  • Color has faded noticeably, especially in the high-traffic lanes.
  • The wood is going grey in patches — that's UV bleaching the exposed fibers.
  • Boards feel rough or are starting to splinter.

Recoat, or strip and start over?

If you catch it while the finish is just thinning, a recoat is a clean-and-reapply — the cheaper, easier path. If you let it go until the stain is peeling in sheets, or it's been painted and the paint is failing, it usually has to come off first, which is a bigger job. That's the real reason to test regularly: catching it early is what keeps deck maintenance cheap.
