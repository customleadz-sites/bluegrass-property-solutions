How often should you wash your house in Kentucky and Tennessee?

Short answer: most homes around here want a house wash every one to two years. The longer answer depends on your trees, your humidity, and which way the dirty wall faces.

It's the most common washing question we get, and the honest answer is that it depends — but not on anything complicated. Once you know the three things that actually drive it, you can look at your own house and get pretty close.

As a working rule for Western Kentucky and Tennessee: a house wash every one to two years, and a driveway or patio whenever it's gone visibly dark. Here's what pushes you toward the shorter end of that.

1. Humidity — and we have plenty of it

The green and grey staining on siding isn't dirt. It's algae and mildew, and it's alive. Living things need moisture, and a Kentucky and Tennessee summer hands them all they need. That's why the same house that stays clean for years in a dry climate needs washing far more often here.

If your siding starts to look dull barely a year after its last wash, you're not imagining it — the humidity is simply growing it back faster.

2. Which way the wall faces

Walk your house and you'll usually find one side is far worse than the others. The north side, and any wall in permanent shade, stays damp longest after rain and dew — so growth takes hold there first and thickest. The sunny south side often looks fine while the north wall is streaked green.

This is normal, and it's why a good wash treats the whole house even if only one side looks bad. The clean sides are just further behind on the same clock.

3. Trees overhead

Tree cover is the single biggest swing factor for driveways and roofs. Under oaks and maples you get shade (more moisture), pollen, sap and leaf tannin — all of which feed staining. A driveway out in the open might go three or four years between washes; the same driveway under a big shade tree can need it every year.

Signs it's overdue right now

  • Green or grey streaks on the siding, especially under the gutters and on shaded walls.
  • A driveway or sidewalk that's gone from concrete-grey to charcoal — or feels slick when it's wet.
  • Black dots or streaks on the roof shingles.
  • A deck or fence that's turned uniformly grey and slippery.

It isn't only about looks

Here's the part people miss: algae and mildew hold moisture against whatever they're growing on. On siding that's mostly cosmetic. On wood — decks, fences, trim — it's the start of rot. Left long enough, a wash-it problem quietly becomes a replace-it problem.

That's the real argument for a regular wash. It's cheap maintenance that keeps a small job from turning into a big one, and it keeps paint and stain lasting the way they're supposed to.

The easy answer

If it's been more than two years, or one side of the house has gone visibly green, it's worth a wash. And if you're not sure, ask — we'd rather tell you it can wait than sell you a wash you don't need yet.
