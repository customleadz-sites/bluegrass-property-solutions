7 signs it's time to repaint your home's exterior

Exterior paint isn't just color — it's the layer keeping water out of your siding and trim. Here's how to tell when that layer is wearing through, before it stops doing its job.

A good exterior paint job should give you a decade or more. But a lot happens in ten Kentucky and Tennessee summers, and paint gives you plenty of warning before it fails outright. Catch the signs early and a repaint is a cosmetic upgrade. Ignore them and it becomes a rot-and-repair job. Here's what to look for.

1. Peeling, flaking or cracking

The obvious one. Once paint is lifting or cracking, the seal is broken and water is getting into the surface underneath. This isn't a "next year" problem — every rain from here makes it worse.

2. Chalking

Run your hand down a sunny wall. If it comes away with a fine, dusty film, that's chalking — the paint's binder breaking down under UV. A little is normal with age; a lot means the coating is spent and no longer shedding water the way it should.

3. Fading, especially uneven fading

Color fade is more than cosmetic. It's a sign the surface has taken years of sun, and the south- and west-facing walls — which fade first — are usually the ones whose protection is furthest gone.

4. Gaps and cracked caulk at the joints

Look where trim meets siding, and around windows and doors. When the caulk has shrunk, cracked or pulled away, those gaps are open doors for water. Fresh caulk and paint close them back up.

5. Bare or grey wood showing through

Any spot where you can see raw or silvered wood is a spot with zero protection. Bare wood drinks water, and wet wood is where rot starts. These areas need priming and painting sooner rather than later.

6. Bubbles or blisters in the paint film

Blistering means moisture is pushing the paint off the wall from behind — often from a coat applied over a damp or dirty surface, or a genuine water problem like a leaking gutter. Worth investigating, because paint alone won't fix the cause.

7. You're about to sell — or you just bought

Not a defect, but a moment. A fresh exterior is one of the highest-return things you can do before listing, and a repaint is far easier before the furniture and landscaping are in the way. If a sale is on the horizon, it's worth pricing now.

Why waiting costs more

Here's the pattern. Paint fails, wood gets exposed, exposed wood takes on water, wet wood rots. A repaint is priced on prep and paint. A repaint with carpentry — replacing rotted trim and siding — is a different number entirely. Every one of the signs above is the house asking you to repaint while it's still just a paint job.
