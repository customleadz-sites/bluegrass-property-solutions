Does pressure washing damage your home?

It can — and most of the horror stories come from one mistake: using full pressure on a surface that was never built to take it. Here's what's safe, what isn't, and how to tell the difference.

Yes, pressure washing can damage your home. That's not a reason to avoid it — it's a reason to make sure whoever's holding the wand knows which surfaces take pressure and which don't. Get that right and washing is one of the safest, highest-return things you can do to a property. Get it wrong and you're paying to repair the thing you paid to clean.

What pressure actually does

A pressure washer can put out anywhere from about 1,300 to 3,000 PSI. On hard, sealed concrete that force is exactly what's needed to blast embedded grime out of the surface. On almost anything softer, that same force is a wrecking tool.

Surfaces that get damaged

  • Vinyl and aluminum siding. High pressure cracks brittle vinyl and dents aluminum — but the bigger problem is invisible: it drives water up under the laps and behind the wall, into insulation and sheathing, where it doesn't dry. You find out a year or two later.
  • Wood — decks, fences, soffits. Pressure carves the soft grain out from between the hard rings and leaves the surface furry and splintered. It also won't take stain evenly afterward.
  • Roof shingles. Full pressure strips the protective granules right off asphalt shingles and voids most roof warranties. Roofs should never be pressure washed.
  • Old mortar and soft brick. Pressure blows out crumbling mortar joints and pits soft historic brick.
  • Windows, screens, light fixtures and seals. All easily cracked, forced or blown out at close range.

Surfaces that are safe to pressure wash

  • Concrete driveways, sidewalks and patios — dense, sealed and going nowhere.
  • Paver and stone hardscape — with sand re-swept into the joints afterward.
  • Solid masonry and retaining walls in good condition.

So how do you clean the rest?

You soft wash it. Soft washing uses low pressure — roughly garden-hose force — plus a cleaning solution that kills the algae and mildew at the root and rinses away. It's what belongs on siding, wood, roofs and anything painted. It's slower and it takes more skill, which is exactly why the cheaper, faster operator reaches for full pressure instead.

Nearly every "the guy who washed my house damaged my siding" story is this: a concrete tool used on a house because it was the only tool on the truck.

How to protect yourself before you hire

You don't need to know PSI numbers. Just ask one question: "Will you be soft washing the siding, or pressure washing it?" The right answer is soft washing the siding and pressure washing the concrete — same visit, two different methods, on purpose. If they say they'll "pressure wash the whole house," ask them to slow down and explain.
